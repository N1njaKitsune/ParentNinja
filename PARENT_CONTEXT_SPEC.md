# Parent Context Spec
## Ambient Education — Content Moments for the Parent App

> This spec defines what contextual information surfaces to parents at each point in the app,
> and the copy/framing to use. Built from `NINJA_SCHOOL_CONTEXT.md`.
> Feed this into screen designs, notification copy, and any parent-facing text.

---

## Philosophy

Parents are physically present but philosophically absent. They watch training, they celebrate belts,
but they don't always understand what their child is internalising — or why it matters.

The app's job is not to educate parents directly, but to contextualise what they already see.
Every moment of confusion or disconnection is an opportunity for a single warm sentence that brings
them back in. This spec maps those moments.

**Rule:** One idea per surface. Never more than 2–3 sentences of context in any one place.

## 1. Belt / Rank Display

**Where it appears:** Student profile hero, lock screen student selector cards

**The moment:** Parent sees their child's current belt name and colour.

**The gap:** Belt names are Japanese and meaningful — but a parent seeing "Honoo Belt" for the
first time just sees a word. The colour and name should carry emotional weight.

**Context copy by tier:**

### Shokyu — Beginner (Shiro, Kiiro, Taiyo, Kazan Belts)

> *"[Child] is in the Beginner tier — Shokyu. This is where every ninja starts: building the
> physical foundations, learning to listen to their body, and discovering what they're capable of.
> Each belt at this stage is a real step forward."*

Belt-specific one-liners (shown beneath belt name):

| Belt | One-liner |
|------|-----------|
| Shiro (White) | The blank page — everything begins here. |
| Kiiro (Yellow) | First light — [child] has taken their first real steps. |
| Taiyo (Orange) | The sun — energy is building and habits are forming. |
| Kazan (Orange/Red) | The volcano — [child] is ready to erupt into the next stage. |

### Chokyu — Intermediate (Honoo, Shin Honoo, Mori, Shin Mori Belts)

> *"[Child] is in the Intermediate tier — Chokyu. The work is getting deeper here. Progress feels
> slower because it is — each milestone now requires more refinement, more consistency, more fire."*

| Belt | One-liner |
|------|-----------|
| Honoo (Red) | Flame — real fire is now present in [child]'s work. |
| Shin Honoo (Red/Grey) | True Flame — the fire is deepening and becoming consistent. |
| Mori (Green) | Forest — growth, rootedness, and expanding range. |
| Shin Mori (Green/Grey) | True Forest — maturity and resilience are taking hold. |

### Jokyu — Advanced (Kage, Shin Kage, Mushin Belts)

> *"[Child] is in the Advanced tier — Jokyu. This is where subtlety lives. The visible effort
> gives way to something quieter and more powerful. You may notice a new kind of calm."*

| Belt | One-liner |
|------|-----------|
| Kage (Grey) | Shadow — [child] moves with subtlety and awareness. |
| Shin Kage (Grey/Black) | True Shadow — approaching mastery, almost invisible in their skill. |
| Mushin (Black/White) | No Mind — action flows without thought. The highest level. |

## 2. Stripe Progress Display

**Where it appears:** Student profile, below belt display

**The moment:** Parent sees a stripe progress indicator (Air, Earth, Water, Fire elements).

**The gap:** Parents may think stripes are just "points to collect." They're not — each element
stripe is instructor-awarded and represents genuine assessed progress.

**Context copy:**

> *"Stripes are never automatic — each one is awarded by [child]'s instructor after they've
> demonstrated real progress. Four elements: Air, Earth, Water, and Fire. All four are needed
> before [child] can move forward."*

**Tier-specific note:**

- **Shokyu:** *"At this stage, [child] earns one stripe per element — four in total to complete this belt."*
- **Chokyu:** *"At this stage, each element takes two rounds — a half-stripe, then a full stripe. Eight milestones per belt. Progress is slower because it's deeper."*
- **Jokyu:** *"At this stage, [child] earns four Air stripes, one at a time. Focused, deliberate, sequential."*

## 3. The Two-Week Challenge

**Where it appears:** Notification, dedicated challenge screen, student profile (when active)

**The moment:** Child has earned all stripes. Belt is ready. But the belt isn't awarded yet —
this is the most important parent moment in the whole system.

**The gap:** Parents receiving a push notification saying "challenge ready" without context will
find it confusing or treat it as a chore. It needs to feel like an honour.

**Notification copy:**

> *"[Child] has earned all their stripes for [Belt Name]. Before their new belt is awarded,
> it's your turn. Set them a Two-Week Challenge — something meaningful, something personal.
> You know them best."*

**Explanation copy (challenge screen):**

> *"Every belt at Ninja School represents more than dojo performance. It includes a personal
> commitment that you and [child] shape together. Think about what would stretch them just
> enough right now — a physical goal, a habit, a mindset target, an act of kindness. Set it,
> support it, and confirm when they've done it. Then the belt is theirs."*

**Completion copy:**

> *"You've confirmed [child]'s challenge. Their [Belt Name] belt is now on its way.
> This moment — the challenge you set and they completed — is part of what that belt means."*

## 4. NEN Aura Display

**Where it appears:** Student profile — alongside or beneath belt display

**The moment:** Parent sees a glowing visual (Aura) with a stage number or phase name.

**The gap:** Without explanation, parents assume this is a game score. It isn't — it's a
reflection of their child's mental development tracked through the Nendō app.

**One-liner (always visible):**

> *"[Child]'s Aura reflects their NEN growth — not physical skill, but mental development:
> focus, perception, and self-regulation."*

**Expandable explanation (tap to learn more):**

> *"NEN is the third pillar of Ninja School, alongside physical training and technique
> refinement. It's the mastery of Attention and Intention — learning to notice what's
> happening inside and around you, and to direct your focus deliberately.*
>
> *The Aura grows as [child] completes Nendō challenges in three areas: Metsuke (perception
> and reading), Tachi (strategy and decisive action), and Mushin (stillness and focus).*
>
> *It isn't a game score. It's the closest thing we have to a picture of [child]'s inner
> development."*

**Aura phase one-liners:**

| Phase | Stages | Parent-facing description |
|-------|--------|--------------------------|
| Kindling | 1–2 | [Child]'s awareness is just beginning to open up. |
| Rising | 3–6 | Focus and perception are growing. Something is building. |
| Burning | 7–10 | The mental work is intensifying. This is where real development happens. |
| Forging | 11–12 | [Child] is developing a rare kind of self-awareness. |
| Beyond | 13 | — |

## 5. Attendance & Consistency

**Where it appears:** Student profile — attendance summary

**The moment:** Parent sees attendance stats.

**The gap:** A raw attendance percentage feels like a school report. It needs to be framed
as what it actually is: a record of showing up.

**Copy approach — narrative over statistics:**

Instead of: *"Attendance: 91%"*
Use: *"[Child] has attended 11 of the last 12 sessions."*

Supporting context (shown small beneath):

> *"Consistency is the foundation of everything at Ninja School. Showing up — even on the
> hard days — is itself a form of practice."*

**Missed session prompt (if applicable):**

> *"[Child] missed a session recently. If you'd like to book a make-up class,
> you can do that from the Attendance screen."*

## 6. Recent Activity

**Where it appears:** Student profile — activity feed

**The moment:** Parent sees what happened in recent sessions.

**The gap:** Without per-grade curriculum data, this section should surface instructor
notes and grading events rather than curriculum labels.

**Format:**

```
[Date] · [Attended / Absent / Late]
[Instructor note if available, or belt/stripe event]
```

**Example entries:**
- *"14 Mar · Attended — Kai earned his Earth stripe. Instructor note: strong focus today."*
- *"7 Mar · Attended"*
- *"28 Feb · Make-up class attended"*

**Note:** Full per-grade curriculum content (what specifically was taught at each belt level)
is not yet documented. Once Section 4 of `NINJA_SCHOOL_CONTEXT.md` is completed, activity
entries can include curriculum context (e.g. "working on kata sequence B this week").

## 7. "How You Can Help" — By Tier

**Where it appears:** Student profile — support section, below recent activity

**The moment:** Parent wants to be involved but doesn't know how.

**The gap:** Generic advice feels like a newsletter. Tier-specific advice feels like it was
written for their child.

### All Tiers — Universal

- **Ask differently.** Instead of *"What did you do in class?"* try *"What did you notice today?"* or *"Was there a moment where you had to really focus?"* This one question reinforces the NEN philosophy every time.
- **The Two-Week Challenge is your moment.** When it arrives, don't rush it. Think about what would genuinely stretch [child] right now.
- **Nendō together.** Even sitting next to [child] while they do a Metsuke or Mushin challenge — or trying one yourself — connects the app to family life rather than leaving it as a solo screen activity.

### Shokyu (Beginner — Belts 1–4)

- Celebrate each stripe openly. At this stage, visible recognition from a parent builds confidence faster than any instructor can.
- Try 30 seconds of "ninja breathing" before homework or bedtime. Inhale for 4, hold for 4, exhale for 4. Simple Mushin practice that costs nothing.
- If [child] is nervous before class, normalise it: every ninja in that room started exactly where they are.

### Chokyu (Intermediate — Belts 5–8)

- Progress slows here by design. The half-stripe system means more milestones before the next belt. Reassure [child] that depth matters more than speed.
- Ask about strategy: *"Did you have to figure something out today?"* This reinforces Tachi thinking at home.
- Let [child] lead on Nendō at this stage — choosing their own challenges builds independence.

### Jokyu (Advanced — Belts 9–11)

- At this level, listen more and direct less. [Child] is developing a quiet kind of self-awareness; your role shifts from cheerleader to witness.
- Acknowledge effort over outcome. Advanced students can become self-critical; a parent who sees the journey rather than just the results is powerful.
- Talk about Mushin in everyday life: moments in sport, music, or conversation where everything just flows. Help [child] recognise those moments outside the dojo.

## 8. NEN Disciplines — Quick Reference for Copy

Use these when writing challenge copy, Nendō prompts, or activity descriptions.

| Discipline | The idea | Parent-friendly prompt |
|------------|----------|----------------------|
| **Metsuke** | The Reading Mind — perception, noticing detail | *"Did you spot anything today that others might have missed?"* |
| **Tachi** | The Striking Mind — strategy, decisive action | *"Was there a moment today where you had to decide quickly?"* |
| **Mushin** | The Still Mind — focus, self-regulation, presence | *"Was there a moment today where everything went quiet and you just... did it?"* |
