# Ninja School — Parent Context Guide

> Everything you need to know about your child's journey at Ninja School, written for parents and guardians.

---

## 1. School Identity

Ninja School is a developmental martial arts and coordination school based in London. It is not a traditional fighting gym — martial arts are the vehicle, but child development is the destination. The school blends Brazilian Jiujitsu, Kickboxing, and Shurikenjutsu with physical conditioning, problem-solving games, and mindfulness practice. What makes the approach distinctive is its holistic philosophy: every session is designed to help young people build healthier relationships, become more in tune with their bodies, develop mental fortitude, and learn to let go of fears. The school operates across three age tiers — Ninja Cubs (ages 3–5), Ninja School (ages 5–10, the core programme), and Secondary (11+) — each shaped to meet children where they are developmentally and carry them forward with purpose.

## 2. TRN Methodology

TRN stands for **TEN, REN, NEN** — three Japanese-inspired principles that form the backbone of Ninja School's teaching system.

**TEN (Containment)** is about physical discipline and control. In practice, it represents the structured, contained element of training — learning to hold your body, manage your energy, and stay composed during physical practice. TEN lives in the dojo; it shapes how students move, spar, and train.

**REN (Refinement)** is about improving technique and deepening skill. Where TEN is about holding things together, REN is about making them sharper. Students working in REN are polishing what they already know — refining a throw, cleaning up a combination, or building consistency in their coordination drills.

**NEN (Noticing)** is the foundational principle underneath everything. NEN is the mastery of Attention and Intention — learning to notice what is happening inside and around you, and to direct your focus deliberately. The school's guiding belief is: "What the mind can do, the body can do." NEN goes beyond intelligence; it is the skill of bending reality into your will through awareness and presence.

Week to week, students experience all three: physical sessions develop TEN and REN, while NEN is woven into every class through mindfulness moments and is extended further through the digital Nendō training module (see Section 5).

## 5. NEN & the Aura System

### What NEN Is

NEN is the philosophical heart of Ninja School. It is the mastery of **Attention** (noticing what is happening) and **Intention** (directing your focus and action with purpose). The school teaches that awareness is the foundation of all skill — physical or mental. "What the mind can do, the body can do."

NEN is practised through three disciplines, each targeting a different aspect of the mind:

**Metsuke — The Reading Mind.** This is about perception: learning to look carefully, notice hidden details, and truly comprehend what is in front of you. In child-friendly terms: "Before a ninja moves, they read. They notice the detail others walk past." Challenges in this area include pattern recognition, spot-the-difference tasks, comprehension, and memory sequences.

**Tachi — The Striking Mind.** This is about strategy and decisive action: knowing when to think carefully and when to act in a flash. "A ninja doesn't just act — they act at the right moment, in the right way." Challenges here include logic puzzles, route-finding, timed quick-fire responses, and decision-making under pressure.

**Mushin — The Still Mind.** This is about focus and self-regulation: learning to quiet the noise, settle, and arrive fully in the present. "When your mind is noisy, nothing works properly. Mushin is the practice of clearing all of that." Challenges include breath pacing, focus holds, guided regulation, and stillness tasks. Critically, this never feels passive — it is always active stillness.

### The Aura System

The Aura is the visual representation of a student's NEN growth. It is a progression system with **13 stages across 4 phases**, moving from a faint warm glow to a deep, powerful presence.

| Phase | Stages | Feel | What It Represents |
|-------|--------|------|-------------------|
| **Kindling** | 1–2 | Warm whites | The aura awakens — faint, warm, barely there. The student is just beginning to notice. |
| **Rising** | 3–6 | Golds and ambers | Energy builds. The aura warms and brightens. Most beginners live here as they develop. |
| **Burning** | 7–10 | Oranges into deep red | The fire intensifies. Something is being forged inside the student. |
| **Forging** | 11–12 | Crimson into violet | The aura begins to transform. A hint of what lies beyond. |
| **Beyond** | 13 | Deep dark violet | Singular. Beyond the system. Those who reach it do not need a name. |

### How Aura Maps to Physical Progression

The Aura system runs alongside the belt system, not instead of it. Belts and stripes track what a student can *do* physically in the dojo. The Aura tracks how a student's *mind* is developing — their perception, decision-making, and self-regulation. From Phase 3 onwards, the student's balance across the three disciplines (Metsuke, Tachi, Mushin) subtly shifts the colour of their aura, making each student's progression feel personal.

### Why It Exists

The Aura system exists because physical skill alone does not capture the full picture of a child's growth. Many children develop focus, resilience, and emotional regulation long before they earn their next belt — and that progress deserves to be seen. The Aura gives students a way to recognise their inner development, not just their physical achievements, and it motivates continued engagement with the mental training side of the programme through the Nendō app.

## 6. Physical Rewards & Recognition

### How Belts and Stripes Are Earned

At each belt level, students earn **element stripes** representing four domains: **Air, Earth, Water, and Fire**. These are physical stripe markers on the belt itself. The rules for earning stripes change as students progress:

**Beginner Tier (Shokyu, Belts 1–4):** Students earn one full stripe per element, in any order. All four elements must be completed to finish a belt — that means 4 stripes per belt.

**Intermediate Tier (Chokyu, Belts 5–8):** Students first earn a half-stripe for each element, then return to earn the full stripe. All four half-stripes must be completed before any full stripe is unlocked. That means 8 stripe milestones per belt (4 halves + 4 fulls).

**Advanced Tier (Jokyu, Belts 9–11):** Students earn four Air stripes per belt, strictly one at a time from left to right. The progression becomes focused and deliberate.

All grading is **instructor-initiated** — stripes are never awarded automatically. Instructors use a dedicated grading tool during Thursday sessions to select students, award stripes, and confirm results with a double-confirmation step.

### The Two-Week Challenge

Once a student has earned all the stripes required for their current belt, they do not receive the new belt immediately. Instead, they enter a **Two-Week Personal Challenge** period:

1. The parent receives a notification that their child is ready for a challenge.
2. The parent sets a personalised two-week challenge — this can be anything meaningful (a physical goal, a habit, a mindset target).
3. The child works on the challenge for two weeks.
4. The parent confirms completion in the app.
5. Only after confirmation is the new belt awarded.

This step ensures that every belt represents more than just dojo performance — it includes a personal commitment that the family has shaped together.

### The Moment of Recognition

During Thursday grading sessions, the instructor enters **Call Out Mode** after awarding stripes. Each student's name and their new awards are presented to the class, giving every child a moment of recognition in front of their peers. A grading receipt is generated for the instructor's records and can be shared with parents.

## 7. How Parents Can Support Each Grade

Because the per-grade curriculum detail is not yet documented (see Section 4), grade-specific suggestions will follow once that content is in place. In the meantime, here is how parents can support their child at every stage:

### All Grades — Universal Support

**Be present in the challenge process.** The Two-Week Challenge is designed to be a family moment. When you receive the notification that your child is ready, take time to think about a challenge that feels meaningful — something that stretches them just enough. It does not have to be physical; it could be a habit, a kindness goal, or a focus target. Your involvement here tells your child that their growth matters beyond the dojo.

**Talk about what they are learning, not just what they are doing.** Instead of "What did you do in class today?", try "What did you notice today?" or "Was there a moment where you had to really focus?" This reinforces the NEN philosophy — that awareness and attention matter as much as technique.

**Encourage the Nendō app.** The digital mental training module (Metsuke, Tachi, Mushin challenges) extends what happens in class into the rest of the week. Even a few minutes of engagement between sessions helps reinforce perception, decision-making, and self-regulation skills. Sitting with your child while they do a challenge — or trying one yourself — can be a powerful way to connect.

### Beginner Tier (Shokyu — Belts 1–4)

- Celebrate each stripe openly — at this stage, every small win builds confidence and momentum.
- Help your child practise stillness at home, even briefly: a 30-second "ninja breathing" moment before homework or bedtime reinforces Mushin.
- If your child is nervous before class, normalise it — remind them that every ninja in the room started exactly where they are.

### Intermediate Tier (Chokyu — Belts 5–8)

- The half-stripe system means progress can feel slower — reassure your child that depth matters more than speed.
- Ask about strategy: "Did you have to figure something out today?" This reinforces Tachi (the Striking Mind) thinking at home.
- Encourage independence in the Nendō app — intermediate students benefit from choosing their own challenges rather than being directed.

### Advanced Tier (Jokyu — Belts 9–11)

- At this level, your child is developing subtlety and self-awareness. The best support is often to listen more and direct less.
- Acknowledge effort over outcome — advanced students can become self-critical, and a parent's recognition of the journey itself is powerful.
- Discuss the idea of Mushin (No Mind) together: moments in sport, music, or daily life where everything just flows. Helping your child recognise those moments outside the dojo deepens their understanding.
